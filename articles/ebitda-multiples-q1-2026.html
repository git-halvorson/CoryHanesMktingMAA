EBITDA Multiples in the Lower Middle Market: Q1 2026 Report

DH David Halvorson · April 11, 2026 · 7 min read

Valuation multiples in the lower middle market ($2M–$15M EBITDA) are not uniform — they vary significantly by sector, growth profile, revenue quality, and deal structure. Understanding where your business falls in the range is the first step to a realistic exit strategy.

Here's what we're seeing in Q1 2026, drawn from active deal activity across our platform and buyer network.

What's Driving the Divergence

Technology Premium Is Accelerating

Businesses with recurring software revenue, proprietary technology, or AI-assisted service delivery are commanding a meaningful premium over comparable human-capital-intensive businesses. A distribution company with a proprietary routing and inventory platform is valued differently than a traditional distributor — even at the same EBITDA level.

PE firms are specifically targeting "technology-enabled" add-ons for their platform companies. If your business has any tech component, make sure your CIM tells that story clearly.

Services Compression Is Real

General business services businesses with undifferentiated offerings and high labor dependency are seeing multiple compression. The culprits: labor cost inflation has reduced margins, making normalized EBITDA harder to defend; buyer competition for these assets has softened; and PE platforms have gotten more selective after overpaying for services roll-ups in 2021–2022.

The Quality Premium Matters More Than Ever

Key finding: The spread between "average quality" and "premium quality" businesses in the same sector has widened to 2–3x turns in most categories. Quality factors — revenue predictability, customer concentration, management depth — now move the needle more than sector selection alone.

What Moves You Toward the Premium Range

  • Recurring / contracted revenue — MRR, ARR, long-term contracts signal predictability
  • Low customer concentration — No customer > 10–15% of revenue is ideal
  • Management depth — A team that can run the business post-close
  • Organic growth — 10%+ CAGR over three years signals market position
  • Gross margin expansion — Improving margins suggest pricing power and operational leverage
  • Clean data room — Organized, audit-ready financials reduce perceived risk

The Process Premium

Multiple ranges above represent what a well-run competitive process achieves. Single-buyer negotiations — where a founder accepts the first serious offer or deals with one strategic buyer — typically close at 1–2 turns below market. The process creates the tension that moves buyers toward the top of their range.

Bottom line: Sector matters, but quality and process matter more. A well-prepared business in a compressing sector, taken through a structured competitive process, will consistently outperform an average business in a hot sector sold quietly to a single buyer.
