Why 80% of Business Owners Leave Money on the Table Without a Competitive Process

DH David Halvorson · April 8, 2026 · 6 min read

When a founder tells me they have "a buyer ready to go" before they've engaged an advisor, I always say the same thing: "That buyer isn't ready to go — they're ready to negotiate."

The most expensive mistake in M&A is confusing a willing buyer with a fair price. Here's the data on why competitive processes consistently outperform single-buyer deals.

The Numbers Don't Lie

Across 500+ closed transactions, businesses sold through a structured, multi-buyer process consistently close at 15–30% higher valuations than comparable businesses sold through a single-buyer negotiation. That's not a slight edge — at a $20M business, 20% is $4M walking out the door.

Example: A $3M EBITDA IT services business sold in a single-buyer negotiation at 6x = $18M. The same business, taken through a competitive process with 8–12 qualified buyers, typically generates LOIs at 7.5–8.5x = $22.5M–$25.5M. The process premium is real and predictable.

Why Single-Buyer Deals Underperform

1. You're Negotiating With No Leverage

In a single-buyer negotiation, the buyer knows they're the only game in town. They have unlimited time to tire you out, uncover weaknesses in due diligence, and renegotiate at the LOI stage. Every data room request is also a negotiating tactic. You have no credible alternative, so you concede.

2. You Don't Know Market Value

Without running a process, you don't know what the market will pay. The first buyer's offer feels like "market" because you have nothing to compare it to. In reality, it's a starting negotiating position — designed to leave room to move only if you push back, which most founders don't.

3. Buyers Compete for Price Only When They Have To

PE firms and strategic acquirers have deal teams whose full-time job is to acquire companies at the best possible price. They know that a seller without a process has no leverage. When they know there are competing LOIs on the table, behavior changes immediately: decision timelines compress, pricing moves up, and terms improve.

What a Competitive Process Looks Like

  1. Prepare the CIM and financial model — 4–6 weeks of preparation before approaching a single buyer
  2. Build the buyer list — 30–80 qualified buyers across PE, strategic, and family office categories
  3. Controlled outreach — Confidential, NDA-gated introductions with deadline-driven process letters
  4. Management presentations — Final-round buyers present their vision; sellers evaluate fit and culture
  5. LOI deadline — All final-round buyers submit offers by the same date, creating genuine competition
  6. Negotiate from strength — With multiple LOIs in hand, you negotiate toward the best combined outcome: price + terms + fit

It's Not Just About Price

A competitive process improves more than the headline number. With multiple buyers competing, you also get:

  • Better deal structure — Less seller note, lower earnout risk, cleaner working capital peg
  • Better terms — Shorter indemnification periods, lower escrow holdbacks, broader reps & warranties coverage
  • Better fit — You can choose the buyer who is the right home for your employees and customers, not just the one who showed up
  • Faster close — Competitive pressure shortens timelines; deals with leverage close faster

The bottom line: A well-run competitive process is the single highest-leverage thing you can do to maximize the outcome of a sale you've spent 20–30 years building toward. The preparation takes time, but it pays for itself many times over.
