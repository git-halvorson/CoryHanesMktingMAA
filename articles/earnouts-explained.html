Earnouts Explained: When They Protect You and When They're a Trap

DH David Halvorson · March 28, 2026 · 10 min read

An earnout is a contractual provision where a portion of the purchase price is contingent on the business achieving specific performance targets post-close. They appear in roughly 30% of lower middle market deals and are among the most negotiated — and most misunderstood — elements of deal structure.

Earnouts can be legitimate tools for bridging valuation gaps. They can also be sophisticated mechanisms that allow buyers to pay you less than you agreed to. Here's how to know which one you're dealing with.

When Earnouts Make Sense (For Both Sides)

The legitimate use case for an earnout is a genuine valuation gap caused by future uncertainty. If your business had an exceptional year and you're projecting continued growth, but the buyer isn't confident projections will materialize, an earnout bridges that gap: you get paid if you're right.

Earnouts also make sense in acquisition structures where the seller will remain actively involved — typically 2–3 years — and the earnout is tied to performance metrics the seller can influence. In these cases, the earnout is less a valuation adjustment and more a performance incentive.

The key test: Can you control the outcome? If the earnout metric is tied to something within your operational control — revenue from your existing customer base, gross margin, your division's EBITDA — it may be workable. If it's tied to combined-company metrics, buyer integration decisions, or capital allocation choices the buyer controls, it's a trap.

Earnout Structures That Favor Sellers

  • Revenue-based, not EBITDA-based. Revenue is harder for buyers to manipulate through cost allocation. EBITDA can be depressed post-close by integration costs, management fees, or shared service allocations that get charged to your business.
  • Short duration. 12–24 months is workable. Three years or more dramatically increases the chance that market conditions, integration decisions, or operational changes will make the targets unreachable for reasons outside your control.
  • Based on your division / book of business only. Combined-company metrics expose you to the buyer's other operations and integration choices.
  • Capped and clearly defined. Know exactly what the maximum payout is and exactly what triggers it. Ambiguity in earnout language always resolves in the buyer's favor during disputes.

Earnout Structures That Favor Buyers (Red Flags)

  • EBITDA-based with shared cost allocations. The buyer can charge management fees, IT overhead, shared services, and integration costs to your business, depressing EBITDA below the threshold.
  • Milestone-based on subjective criteria. "Successful integration," "customer retention at buyer's discretion," or "operational KPIs to be determined post-close" are blank checks for non-payment.
  • No non-interference provisions. If the buyer can change your pricing, redirect your sales team, switch your key customers to their platform, or terminate key employees — all of which would kill your earnout — and there's no contractual protection against it, you're exposed.
  • Long duration (3+ years). Over three years, the original conditions almost certainly won't hold. New management, market shifts, and integration changes will complicate attribution.

How to Negotiate Earnout Protections

RiskProtection to Request
Buyer charges costs to your divisionCap on allocated overhead / management fees
Buyer changes your go-to-marketNon-interference covenant for earnout period
Buyer redirects key customersCustomer retention protection / approval rights
Metric manipulationIndependent accountant determination of earnout
Ambiguous targetsSpecific, objectively measurable definitions
Dispute resolutionBinding arbitration with CPA neutral, not litigation

The Alternative: Push for More Cash at Close

The best earnout protection is minimizing the earnout. In a competitive process, sellers have leverage to push buyers toward higher upfront cash and smaller contingent payments. Every dollar moved from the earnout column to the close column is a dollar you don't have to earn twice.

If a buyer insists on an earnout as a material part of the structure, it often signals either (a) genuine uncertainty about projections, in which case you should revisit your valuation basis, or (b) a negotiating strategy to reduce risk on their side by transferring it to yours.

Bottom line: Earnouts are not inherently bad, but they require specific protections to be workable for sellers. If you can't get those protections in the LOI stage, assume the earnout won't pay — and price your acceptance decision accordingly.
