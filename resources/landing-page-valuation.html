The Valuation Problem

The Number You Have in Mind. The Number a Buyer Will Offer.

Most owners approaching an exit have done mental math. They know the industry multiple. They know their earnings. The gap between that estimate and the offer they receive in a formal process is almost never the result of dishonesty — it's the result of information asymmetry.

Buyers enter diligence with a detailed methodology. They reconstruct EBITDA from source records using their own normalization criteria. They identify risk factors that move the multiple within the industry range. They set working capital targets that may differ significantly from what the seller assumed. By the time a formal offer is made, the buyer has run a detailed analysis. The seller, often, has not.

The worksheet gives you a buyer's lens before you need one.

Multiple Drivers

What Moves Your Multiple Within the Industry Range

Buyers don't apply a single industry multiple. They start with the range and adjust based on factors they can observe directly. Understanding these factors — and where your business stands on each — is the core purpose of the worksheet.

Factor Multiple Expansion Multiple Compression
Revenue Quality +0.5x Recurring or contractual revenue, high renewal rates −0.5x Transactional revenue, project-dependent, high concentration

Common Questions

What Owners Ask Before Downloading

How accurate is the valuation range the worksheet produces?

The worksheet is a planning tool, not a formal valuation opinion. It uses current lower middle market transaction data by industry and applies the risk adjustment factors buyers commonly use. The range it produces is a reasonable starting point for understanding where a formal offer might land — not a guarantee. A formal business valuation requires a certified appraiser or investment banker with access to comparable transaction data.

My business is profitable but I've run personal expenses through it. Does that affect the analysis?

Yes — this is exactly what the EBITDA normalization step addresses. Expenses that are personal in nature, non-recurring, or above-market owner compensation should be added back to calculate normalized EBITDA. The worksheet walks through the most common categories. The key is that each add-back needs to be documentable — buyers will ask for supporting detail on every adjustment.

My industry isn't on the list. What should I do?

Use the industry that most closely resembles your business model. If your business spans multiple categories, use the one that best reflects your primary revenue driver. The industry multiple is a starting point — the compression and expansion factors are what refine the output to your specific situation.

I'm not planning to sell for 3+ years. Is this relevant for me?

Yes — and arguably more relevant. Owners who are 3 or more years from a planned exit have the most time to address the factors that drive multiple compression. The worksheet is most valuable as a planning tool, not a pre-sale checklist. Knowing where you stand now gives you time to act on it.
