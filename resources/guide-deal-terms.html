Seller Reference Guide
The Deal Behind the Deal:
Transaction Terms for Business Owners
Prepared by: Horizon M&A Advisors
Target audience: Business owners planning an exit in 1–3 years
Transaction scope: Lower middle market ($10M–$100M revenue)
Confidential Prepared for the exclusive use of the recipient. Not for distribution. Does not constitute legal or financial advice.

In a lower middle market sale process, the headline purchase price and the amount the seller actually receives — at closing and over time — are frequently different numbers. The gap lives in the deal structure: the terms that govern how the price is paid, under what conditions, over what timeline, and with what contingencies.

This guide explains the transaction terms that most directly affect seller economics. It is not a legal document and does not constitute legal or financial advice. Its purpose is to ensure that when these terms appear in an offer, you understand what you are looking at before you respond to it.

Purchase Price Structure

Section 1 of 5
Enterprise Value vs. Equity Value High Importance
Enterprise value (EV) is the total value of the business — the number buyers quote in offers and the press covers in deal announcements. Equity value is what the seller actually receives after adjustments: adding excess cash, subtracting funded debt, and adjusting for working capital. In most lower middle market transactions, the seller's proceeds equal equity value, not enterprise value.
Favorable
Excess cash retained by seller; minimal funded debt; working capital at or above the agreed target at closing
Negotiate Carefully
Understand exactly which liabilities are being deducted and which assets are being included in the "cash-free, debt-free" definition
Watch For
Broad debt definitions that capture deferred revenue, accrued liabilities, or unfunded pension obligations not traditionally classified as debt
Illustrative Example
Enterprise value: $12,000,000. Less: funded debt ($800K), working capital shortfall ($200K), escrow holdback ($720K). Cash at closing: $10,280,000. The seller's check is 14% below the headline number — before earnout, rep & warranty claims, or post-close adjustments.
Closing Consideration vs. Deferred Consideration High Importance
The total purchase price is typically split between consideration paid at closing and consideration paid after closing. Deferred consideration includes earnouts, equity rollover, and seller notes. Only closing consideration is certain — deferred consideration is contingent on future events, business performance, or the buyer's financial health.
Favorable Structure
80%+ of total consideration paid at closing in cash; deferred consideration tied to objective, auditable metrics
Acceptable with Protections
60–79% at closing; earnout tied to EBITDA or revenue with clear measurement criteria and dispute resolution process
Unfavorable
Less than 60% at closing; large seller note; earnout tied to subjective criteria or buyer's internal reporting

Earnouts

Section 2 of 5
Earnout Structure & Mechanics High Importance
An earnout is a deferred payment contingent on the business achieving specified performance targets — typically revenue or EBITDA — over a defined period after closing. Earnouts are used when buyer and seller disagree on the value of near-term growth projections. They allow the seller to receive additional consideration if the projections prove accurate, while protecting the buyer if they don't.
Seller-Favorable Design
Revenue-based metric (easier to measure); short duration (12 months); low threshold relative to recent performance; buyer covenant to not starve the business of resources
Acceptable with Protections
EBITDA-based metric with agreed accounting treatment; 18–24 months; threshold at or below last full year actual performance; independent accountant for dispute resolution
Exercise Caution
Metrics subject to buyer's internal accounting decisions; 3+ year duration; targets based on projections that exceed historical performance; no buyer obligation to fund growth investment
Key Negotiation Points
Push for: (1) objective, auditable measurement criteria; (2) buyer covenants to maintain adequate resources; (3) acceleration upon change of control; (4) independent accountant for disputes; (5) carved-out categories of costs the buyer cannot add to reduce earnout EBITDA.
When Earnouts Are Appropriate (and When They Aren't) Context-Dependent
Earnouts are appropriate when there is a genuine valuation gap driven by near-term growth projections that the historical financials don't yet support — the seller believes the business will grow significantly, and the earnout captures value if that growth materializes. Earnouts are not appropriate as a substitute for buyers underwriting the current, demonstrated earnings of the business.
Appropriate Use
Business has signed contracts not yet generating revenue; recent contract wins not reflected in trailing EBITDA; clear, near-term growth event (product launch, geographic expansion)
Inappropriate Use
Buyer applying earnout to demonstrated, historical earnings they could verify in diligence; earnout used to bridge a valuation gap created by the buyer's own conservative normalization
Advisor Note
If a buyer is using an earnout to avoid paying for what the business demonstrably earns today, that is a different transaction structure than the headline suggests. Understand which situation applies before accepting.

Working Capital Adjustment

Section 3 of 5
Working Capital Target & Peg Mechanism High Importance
Working capital — typically accounts receivable plus inventory minus accounts payable — is expected to transfer with the business at closing at a "target" level. If actual working capital at closing is below the target, the purchase price is adjusted down dollar-for-dollar. If above, the price adjusts up. The target is negotiated at LOI, before formal diligence confirms what the actual figure will be.
Favorable
Target set at trailing 12-month average of actual working capital; clear definition of included/excluded items; seller-friendly dispute resolution (neutral accountant, not buyer's auditor)
Watch Carefully
Target set at trailing 3-month average (can be inflated by seasonal highs); definition of current liabilities includes items the seller considers non-current
Unfavorable
Target set above historical average; broad definition of current liabilities; buyer's auditor serves as dispute resolver; adjustment applied to the full closing consideration rather than just the escrow
Why This Matters at LOI Stage
The working capital target is agreed at LOI — before formal diligence. Buyers will have modeled the target carefully. Sellers who haven't run their own analysis consistently accept targets set in the buyer's favor. A target set 15% above your trailing average requires you to fund the difference out of the closing proceeds — dollar for dollar.

Escrow, Indemnification & Representations

Section 4 of 5
Escrow & Indemnification Holdback High Importance
A portion of the closing consideration — typically 8–15% — is held in escrow for a defined period (12–24 months) as security for the seller's indemnification obligations. If the buyer makes a valid indemnification claim during that period, the amount is deducted from escrow before the balance is released to the seller. Escrow is not a penalty — it is a mechanism for resolving legitimate post-close claims without litigation.
Favorable Terms
Holdback: 8% or less; duration: 12 months; release: automatic upon expiration absent pending claims; claims threshold (basket): 0.75% or more of purchase price
Market Standard
Holdback: 10%; duration: 18 months; basket: 0.5–0.75%; cap on indemnification: equal to purchase price for fundamental reps, holdback amount for general reps
Unfavorable
Holdback: 15%+; duration: 24 months; no basket (first dollar indemnification); cap on indemnification equals full purchase price for general reps
Representations & Warranties High Importance
In a purchase agreement, the seller makes a set of representations and warranties — factual statements about the business that are true as of closing. If any representation is materially false, the buyer may have an indemnification claim against the seller. The scope and survival period of these reps directly affect the seller's exposure after closing.
Seller-Favorable
Reps qualified by "knowledge" and "materiality"; fundamental reps (title, authority) survive longer but limited in number; general reps survive 12–18 months; narrow definition of "material adverse effect"
Negotiate Qualification
Work with transaction counsel to add knowledge qualifiers to reps covering matters outside the seller's direct knowledge; limit reps to periods within the seller's tenure
Watch For
Unqualified reps covering historical periods before seller's control; broad "material adverse effect" definitions; environmental reps with no knowledge qualifier; reps that survive beyond 24 months for general matters
Rep & Warranty Insurance
In transactions above approximately $15M of enterprise value, rep and warranty insurance (RWI) is increasingly common. RWI allows the buyer to make indemnification claims against an insurer rather than the seller's escrow, often reducing or eliminating the seller's holdback obligation. Ask your advisor whether RWI is appropriate for your transaction.

Equity Rollover & Seller Notes

Section 5 of 5
Equity Rollover Situationally Important
In transactions involving private equity buyers, sellers are frequently asked to "roll" a portion of their equity — typically 10–20% of their proceeds — into the new, post-acquisition entity. The rollover is a second bite at the apple: if the business grows under the PE firm's ownership and is eventually sold, the seller participates in the exit at the higher value. The risk is that the second bite may be worth less than expected — or may never materialize.
Favorable Terms
Tag-along rights (seller can exit alongside PE in future sale); anti-dilution protection; information rights; defined exit timeline; rollover valued at same basis as PE investment
Negotiate These Points
Governance rights (board observer seat); drag-along limitations; put option allowing seller to force liquidity after a defined period; clear definition of "exit" for acceleration purposes
Exercise Caution
No tag-along rights; rollover equity subordinated to PE preferred; no information rights; mandatory rollover with no negotiation on percentage or terms
Seller Notes Situationally Important
A seller note (or seller financing) involves the seller lending a portion of the purchase price to the buyer, to be repaid over time with interest. Seller notes are common in lower middle market transactions where the buyer's financing package doesn't fully cover the purchase price. They are not equivalent to cash at closing — they are an unsecured loan to the buyer, with the associated credit risk.
Favorable Structure
Short duration (3–4 years); market interest rate (prime + 1–2%); security interest in business assets if possible; prepayment without penalty; acceleration upon change of control
Acceptable
5-year duration; reasonable interest rate; subordinated to senior lender; clear repayment schedule with defined default provisions
Unfavorable
7+ years; below-market interest rate; entirely subordinated with no security; seller note used as primary financing mechanism rather than a gap-fill

Quick Reference: Transaction Terminology

Reference
Term Definition
Adjusted EBITDA Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization, normalized to remove non-recurring, owner-specific, or non-operating items. The earnings figure buyers use to underwrite and apply a purchase multiple.
Basket / Deductible The minimum threshold of indemnification claims required before the seller owes anything. Claims below the basket threshold are the buyer's responsibility. A "tipping basket" means the full amount is owed once the threshold is crossed; a "true deductible" means only the excess over the threshold is owed.
Cash-Free, Debt-Free Standard assumption in lower middle market transactions: the buyer acquires the business without the seller's excess cash and without assuming the seller's funded debt. Both items are adjusted in the enterprise-to-equity-value bridge.
Closing Condition A requirement that must be satisfied before the transaction can close. If a closing condition is not met, the buyer may have the right to terminate or renegotiate. Common conditions include regulatory approvals, third-party consents, and material adverse change provisions.
Drag-Along Rights PE buyer's right to require minority equity holders (including a rolling seller) to participate in a future sale on the same terms as the majority. Limits the seller's ability to block a future exit.
EBITDA Multiple The ratio of enterprise value to adjusted EBITDA. The primary valuation metric in lower middle market M&A. A business trading at 6x EBITDA with $2M of adjusted EBITDA has an enterprise value of $12M.
Letter of Intent (LOI) A non-binding document that outlines the key economic and structural terms of a proposed transaction. The LOI is signed before formal diligence and establishes the framework for the definitive purchase agreement. Most LOIs include an exclusivity period during which the seller cannot negotiate with other buyers.
Material Adverse Change (MAC) A provision allowing the buyer to terminate the transaction if a material adverse change in the business occurs between signing and closing. MAC definitions are heavily negotiated — broader definitions favor buyers, narrower definitions favor sellers.
Rep & Warranty Insurance (RWI) Insurance purchased (typically by the buyer) that allows indemnification claims to be made against an insurer rather than the seller's escrow. Increasingly common in transactions above ~$15M of enterprise value. Can substantially reduce the seller's holdback obligation.
Tag-Along Rights A rolling seller's right to participate alongside the PE buyer in a future sale of the business on the same terms. Protects minority equity holders from being left behind in an exit.
Working Capital Peg The negotiated target level of working capital that should be in the business at closing. Sets the baseline for post-close working capital adjustments. Established at LOI — typically before the seller has run their own working capital analysis.

Negotiation Principles for Sellers

Advisory Notes
1
Run your own working capital analysis before the LOI The working capital target is negotiated at LOI — before formal diligence. Buyers have already modeled it. If you haven't, you are negotiating without information against a counterparty who has it.
2
Understand what you are accepting before you sign the LOI The LOI is non-binding on price — but signing it begins the exclusivity period and creates significant momentum toward the buyer's proposed structure. Changes after signing are possible but create friction and signal weakness.
3
Evaluate earnouts on their mechanics, not their headline An earnout is only as valuable as the criteria used to measure it. Before accepting an earnout, understand exactly how the measurement metric is defined, who calculates it, and what remedies you have if you disagree with the result.
4
Negotiate escrow terms alongside the purchase price The escrow amount, duration, basket threshold, and cap are deal terms, not boilerplate. A 10% holdback on a $15M transaction is $1.5M not available to you for 12–24 months. Negotiate it as if it were part of the price — because economically, it is.
5
If there is equity rollover, negotiate governance rights As a minority equity holder in the post-acquisition entity, you have limited rights unless they are explicitly documented. At minimum, negotiate tag-along rights, information rights, and clarity on the exit timeline.
6
Transaction counsel and M&A advisor are not interchangeable Your transaction attorney reviews and negotiates the purchase agreement. Your M&A advisor structures the process, manages buyer relationships, and advises on business and economic terms. Both roles are necessary — neither substitutes for the other.
