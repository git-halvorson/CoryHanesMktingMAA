Section 2 — Revenue Quality
Scoring Key

1
Revenue Mix
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
50% or more of revenue is recurring in nature — subscriptions, retainers, maintenance agreements, or multi-year service contracts with defined renewal terms. Buyers can underwrite future cash flows with confidence. 25–50% of revenue is recurring. A meaningful portion is project-based or variable. Buyers will apply some discount for the unpredictability, but a clear recurring foundation exists to build on. Revenue is primarily transactional or project-based. Each year requires rebuilding the top line from a near-zero base. Buyers treat this as a materially higher-risk profile and underwrite accordingly.
2
Customer Concentration
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
No single customer exceeds 10% of revenue. The top three customers collectively represent less than 30% of total revenue. The customer base is diversified and does not present single-point-of-failure risk. The top customer represents 10–20% of revenue, or the top three represent 30–45% collectively. Buyers will flag this in diligence but will typically proceed with appropriate structural protections. One customer exceeds 20% of revenue, or the top three collectively exceed 45%. This concentration profile triggers significant buyer concern and commonly results in earnouts, escrow requirements, or reduced headline valuation.
3
Contract Status
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
The majority of revenue is governed by written contracts with defined terms, pricing, and renewal provisions. Contracts are assignable to a buyer without requiring customer re-approval or renegotiation. Some written contracts exist but coverage is inconsistent. A mix of formal agreements and informal arrangements reduces the certainty of revenue transfer and may require buyer-funded customer re-engagement post-close. Revenue relationships are primarily informal or at-will. No written contracts govern the majority of the customer base. Buyers must treat all revenue as potentially at-risk at the time of ownership transfer.
4
Renewal & Retention Rate
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
Customer retention is 85% or higher on an annual basis, and this history is documented in a form that can be presented and substantiated during buyer due diligence. Retention demonstrates the durability of customer relationships. Retention falls in the 70–85% range. Churn exists but is manageable. Buyers will require explanation of customer exits and may apply modest risk adjustments to projected revenue in their underwriting models. Retention is below 70%, or customer retention has not been formally tracked. In the absence of data, buyers will assume the most adverse scenario and discount revenue projections to reflect unquantified churn risk.
5
Revenue Growth Trend
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
Revenue has grown organically at 10% or more per year for two or more consecutive years. The growth trend is consistent and is not attributable to one-time events, single customer additions, or acquisitions. Revenue growth is flat to modest — in the 0–10% range. The business is stable but does not demonstrate the trajectory that commands premium pricing. Buyers will underwrite conservatively against this profile. Revenue is declining or exhibiting significant year-over-year variability. Buyers will require a substantive explanation, apply downside protection in deal structure, and are unlikely to support a premium multiple absent a compelling turnaround narrative.
