What Is EBITDA?

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It is the starting point for M&A valuation because it approximates operating cash flow before financing and accounting decisions. If your CPA has already given you an EBITDA figure, enter it directly in Section 3 and skip this section.

Owner & Related-Party Compensation Add-Backs

The most significant and most commonly misunderstood normalization adjustment. Buyers add back the difference between what you actually pay yourself and what a market-rate replacement CEO/GM would cost. If you underpay yourself, there may be no add-back — or even a negative adjustment.

Owner #1 — Primary Owner / Operating Principal
Include: W-2 salary + owner distributions taken as compensation + auto allowance + health insurance premiums paid by the business + any other personal benefits. Do not include returns on investment or rent paid to you separately.
What would a hired CEO, President, or General Manager cost to perform your operational role? For businesses under $20M revenue, typically $150K–$300K. Use industry salary data or ask your advisor.

One-Time & Non-Recurring Items

Expenses that hit the P&L in the period but are genuinely non-recurring — they would not occur in a normalized year of operations. Buyers are skeptical of this category and will probe each item. Document every add-back with invoices or clear explanations.

Buyer Scrutiny Warning

One-time add-backs are the most frequently challenged category in a Quality of Earnings. Buyers and their QoE advisors will ask for backup documentation on every item. Avoid overstating this category — overly aggressive one-time add-backs destroy credibility at exactly the wrong moment in a process.

One-Time Item Amount
Legal Fees — Non-Recurring Litigation or Transactions Attorney fees for a one-time dispute, settlement, or corporate restructuring. Ongoing legal expenses are NOT addable.
Severance & Restructuring Costs One-time severance payments, workforce restructuring, or plant/office closures not expected to recur
Lease Termination / Relocation Expenses Penalties, moving costs, or write-offs associated with a specific lease termination or office relocation
Non-Recurring Consulting / Advisory Fees Special project consultants, one-time advisory engagements, or M&A preparation costs not part of normal operations
Capital Expenditures Expensed (not capitalized) Equipment or improvements that were immediately expensed under Section 179 or bonus depreciation rather than capitalized. Add back if truly non-recurring infrastructure investment.
COVID / Pandemic-Related One-Time Costs PPE purchases, sanitation, temporary facility changes. Note: PPP loan forgiveness income should be excluded from revenue, not added back here.
Litigation Settlement / Damages Paid One-time settlement payment or damages award. Only add back if clearly non-recurring and fully resolved.
Other One-Time Items Any other non-recurring expense with clear documentation and rationale

What Buyers Will Do With This Number

A buyer's Quality of Earnings advisor will conduct their own analysis of your add-backs. Items with clean documentation — invoices, payroll records, tax returns — survive scrutiny. Items without backup tend to be partially or fully disallowed. The goal of this worksheet is to help you identify and document your add-backs before a buyer's team does it for you — when you have less leverage to defend them.

The Gap That Matters Most

The difference between the Low and High estimates is almost always larger than owners expect — often $1M–$5M or more on a typical lower middle market business. That gap is not random. It is determined by specific, addressable factors: revenue quality, customer concentration, owner dependency, growth trajectory, and financial documentation. These are precisely the gaps Horizon identifies in a pre-market preparation engagement — before a buyer's team finds them in diligence.
