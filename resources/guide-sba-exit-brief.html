Section One
The Policy Context: What the SBA Actually Does
Horizon M&A Advisors
Market Intelligence Brief · April 2026

For most business owners, news about the SBA budget sounds like just another fight in Washington. For anyone planning to sell a business in the next few years, it is considerably more consequential than that.

What the SBA Quietly Does for Deal Flow

The Small Business Administration runs two programs that matter most to lower middle market deal-making: the 7(a) loan program and the 504 program. Together, they support roughly $30 billion of small-business lending per year. They do not lend money directly — they guarantee a portion of loans made by banks and approved lenders, which is what makes lenders willing to extend credit to business buyers who lack the balance sheet of a large corporation.

When those guarantees are strong, a first-time buyer or a lightly capitalized search fund can put 10–15% down and borrow the rest at competitive rates. When those guarantees are weakened — through budget cuts, rule changes, or program disruption — lenders tighten. Advance rates fall, pricing rises, and some buyers simply disappear from the market.

This is not theoretical. We have already seen the mechanism play out.

What Has Already Changed

The proposed budget cut is not the only development affecting SBA lending. Recent rule changes have already tightened the program in meaningful ways — including changes that complicated minority-investor structures and added friction to the underwriting process. Industry data shows these changes have already added approximately 30 days to SBA-backed closing timelines, with valuations and deal volume softening in the sub-$5M revenue range most dependent on SBA financing.

A 67% funding cut is not a trim. It is a structural reduction in the program's ability to operate at scale. The full budget has not passed — Congress will negotiate — but the direction of travel is clear: less government support for small-business credit, not more.

Important Distinction

This is a budget proposal, not a final law. Congress will negotiate. The SBA still operates today. What has changed is the risk profile of an exit plan that assumes future SBA-backed buyers will be as available and aggressive as they have been historically.

Why This Brief Exists

Most advisors will not connect federal budget news to the actual mechanics of how your business gets valued and sold. Having spent more than a decade on the buy side at Intel — where we evaluated acquisitions ranging from small technology firms to large strategic deals — I learned that macro policy almost always reaches the deal table eventually. The question is whether you see it coming.

This brief is an attempt to translate the policy signal into practical deal-level consequence, and to identify the steps owners in the $5–$50M range can take now rather than after the market has already shifted.

Section Two
Deal Mechanics: How This Shows Up at the Table
Horizon M&A Advisors
Market Intelligence Brief · April 2026

Strip away the politics and look at deal mechanics. A material reduction in SBA lending capacity translates into three specific changes in the buyer pool for businesses in the $1M–$20M enterprise value range.

1
Less leverage available to individual and search-fund buyers

The SBA 7(a) program has enabled buyers to put 10–15% down and borrow the rest. With fewer or weaker guarantees, banks tighten underwriting standards, reduce advance rates, and increase pricing on small-business acquisition loans. Buyers who were previously qualified at a certain offer price can no longer support that number with the same debt structure.

2
First-time buyers and lightly capitalized acquirers exit the market

A large portion of the buyer pool for $1M–$5M EBITDA businesses consists of individual buyers, entrepreneurial search funds, and management teams using SBA-backed debt to get deals done. Remove or significantly reduce that financing option, and these buyers either disappear from auctions entirely or arrive with substantially lower offers. The competitive tension that drives prices up weakens at precisely the wrong moment for sellers.

3
Market power shifts to PE firms, family offices, and strategic acquirers

Well-capitalized sponsors and strategic buyers with strong balance sheets do not depend on SBA programs. They can still get deals done. But they can also afford to be selective — and without competition from SBA-backed individual buyers, they face less competitive pressure. The natural result: lower offers, more aggressive deal structure demands, and more leverage in negotiation.

Buy-Side Perspective

In acquisition processes I observed at Intel, the presence or absence of competing bidders was the single biggest driver of final price. When multiple qualified buyers competed for the same asset, prices moved up quickly. When the qualified buyer pool was thin — for any reason — offers came in lower and terms were less seller-friendly. A shrinking SBA-backed buyer pool has the same effect as a smaller competitive process: it reduces the competitive tension that sellers depend on to maximize value.

Who Is Most Exposed

Higher Exposure

  • Businesses with $1M–$3M EBITDA — most dependent on SBA-financed individual buyers
  • Owners planning to sell in the next 12–24 months with no process started
  • Businesses with customer concentration, owner dependency, or other risk factors that make PE/strategic buyers less enthusiastic
  • Owners expecting a clean, all-cash close with no seller paper

Lower Exposure

  • Businesses with $4M+ EBITDA — more attractive to PE platforms not relying on SBA debt
  • Owners in sectors with strong strategic buyer interest (IT services, SaaS, health tech) — these buyers have their own capital
  • Businesses with clean financials, recurring revenue, and documented management depth
  • Owners who are 2–3 years out and can address gaps before going to market

Section Three
The Valuation & Structure Impact
Horizon M&A Advisors
Market Intelligence Brief · April 2026

When buyers lose access to leverage, one of two things happens: they lower their offer, or they ask the seller to carry more of the risk. In a tightening SBA environment, owners in the $5–$20M EV range should expect to see both.

How Reduced SBA Capacity Affects Valuation

The relationship between available leverage and enterprise value is direct. When buyers can borrow 80–85% of the purchase price at competitive rates, they can bid more aggressively for a business. When that leverage is unavailable or more expensive, the math on what a buyer can responsibly offer changes — and offers come in lower.

We have already seen early signals of this dynamic. Following recent SBA rule changes, industry sources have reported lower valuations and longer closing times in the sub-$5M revenue segment most dependent on SBA financing. A 67% funding cut would extend that pressure upmarket.

The Assumption Worth Stress-Testing

Many lower middle market owners carry an implicit assumption that is worth examining: "When I'm ready to sell, buyers will just get an SBA loan." If that assumption is embedded in your exit timeline or expected valuation, it is now materially riskier than it was 12 months ago.

The Private Credit Offset

Private credit funds and non-bank lenders have been expanding aggressively in the lower middle market, offering flexible capital to fill gaps left by traditional bank lending. This is genuine. But private credit comes at a price: higher spreads, tighter covenants, and terms that generally favor the lender over the business being acquired. For a seller, a buyer financing with expensive private credit may be less able to pay a full price than a buyer using low-cost SBA-backed debt. The pool does not disappear — it reprices.

Section Four
What Owners Should Do Now
Horizon M&A Advisors
Market Intelligence Brief · April 2026

None of us controls federal budgets, interest rates, or the political cycle. What owners do control is how prepared they are when policy changes reach the deal table. These four steps are worth considering regardless of your timeline.

1
Re-examine your exit timing with fresh assumptions

If your target window is 2–5 years, the calculus may have shifted. Not because a sale should be rushed — it should not — but because the preparation runway that drives outcome quality takes 12–24 months to run properly. Owners who are 2–3 years out have time. Owners who are 12 months out have less margin for error in a tightening credit environment.

2
Broaden your mental model of "the buyer"

If you have been picturing an SBA-financed individual buyer as your likely acquirer, expand that picture. PE platform companies, family offices, strategic acquirers, and regional operators with capital do not depend on SBA programs. These buyers are more selective, but they are also better capitalized and can close. The question is whether your business is positioned to attract them.

3
Focus on the fundamentals that survive credit tightening

When lenders tighten, buyers get pickier — and the businesses that still attract competitive offers share specific characteristics: predictable, recurring revenue; clean, auditable financials; documented management depth; and diversified customer concentration. These factors matter in any market. In a tighter credit environment, they become the difference between a competitive process and a single-offer negotiation.

4
Stress-test your expected valuation

The question worth sitting with: "If buyers face higher borrowing costs and a shallower lending market, what does that do to the number I'm expecting?" It is far better to think through that scenario now — with 18–24 months of runway — than in the middle of a live process, when you have already disclosed financials and lost negotiating leverage.

What This Does Not Mean

This brief is not an argument that the market is broken or that now is uniquely bad to sell. It is an argument for preparation. Well-prepared businesses with strong fundamentals and the right advisor access will continue to close excellent transactions in this environment. The gap between prepared and unprepared sellers — in any market — is where outcome quality is determined.

A Note on Timing and What We Are Watching

The proposed SBA budget cut remains a proposal, subject to Congressional negotiation. Several outcomes are plausible: a full cut, a partial cut, a maintained budget with program-level rule changes, or a continuation of the status quo. Horizon is monitoring this closely. What is already clear — from rule changes that preceded this proposal — is that the direction of travel for small-business credit is tighter, not looser.

For owners in the planning window, that trend is worth understanding regardless of how the specific budget negotiation resolves. The structural shift toward private credit as the dominant non-bank financing source for small business acquisitions is already underway.

The Horizon Perspective

Preparation is the only variable a seller fully controls. Our model — valuation benchmarking, gap analysis, buyer research, and CIM development before approaching a single buyer — was designed precisely for markets where the qualified buyer pool is selective. If you are 1–3 years from a potential exit and want an honest read on how your business looks to the buyers who will actually be competing for it, that conversation is worth having now.
