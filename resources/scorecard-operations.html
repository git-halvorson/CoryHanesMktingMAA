Operations & Key Person Risk Scorecard
An assessment of the five operational factors buyers examine to determine whether a business can sustain performance after ownership transfers — and whether the value resides in the company or in the owner.
Section 3 — Operations & Key Person Risk
Scoring Key

1
Owner Dependency
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
The business can operate at full performance for 90 or more consecutive days without the owner present. Customer relationships, vendor relationships, and key decisions are managed by staff. The owner's role is defined, scoped, and documentable. The business can function operationally in the owner's absence but customer relationships, key vendor negotiations, or high-stakes decisions remain owner-dependent. A transition plan exists in concept but has not been documented or tested. The business is materially dependent on the owner's daily involvement. Customers buy from the owner personally, key operational knowledge is not documented, and the business would face measurable disruption within 30 days of the owner's departure.
2
Management Team Depth
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
A capable management team is in place with defined roles, documented responsibilities, and the ability to run day-to-day operations independently. Buyers would view this team as retainable and capable of executing a transition without the founder. Some management depth exists but is uneven. One or two key roles are well-covered; others are owner-dependent or occupied by staff who would be difficult to retain through a change of ownership. Buyers will factor transition risk into their offer structure. No meaningful management layer exists below the owner. All significant decisions and relationships run through the owner exclusively. This profile limits the buyer pool to those willing to assume a full operational role and dramatically compresses the achievable multiple.
3
Process Documentation
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
Standard operating procedures for core business functions — sales, delivery, client onboarding, operations, and finance — are documented in sufficient detail to be executed by a competent employee without owner involvement or interpretation. Some processes are documented, but coverage is incomplete. Critical functions rely on tribal knowledge held by the owner or a small number of key employees. Buyers will identify this gap during operational diligence and may use it to negotiate post-close consulting arrangements. Core operational processes are entirely undocumented. The business runs on institutional memory and informal communication. Buyers face significant integration risk and will price accordingly — or decline to proceed with a full-price offer.
4
Employee Stability & Retention Risk
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
Key employees have multi-year tenure, are compensated at market rates, and have no known departure risk. Retention agreements or stay bonuses for critical personnel have been considered. The workforce is stable and would transfer cleanly to a new owner. Most employees are stable but one or two key individuals present retention uncertainty — whether due to compensation gaps, personal loyalty to the owner, or undisclosed offers. Buyers will identify and stress-test these risks during management interviews. Significant employee turnover, compensation below market, or key personnel whose departure would materially impact the business. Buyers will view this as an operational risk and will either reprice the deal or require retention mechanisms funded by the seller at close.
5
Systems & Technology Infrastructure
Green  / 2 pts Yellow  / 1 pt Red  / 0 pts
Core operational systems — ERP, CRM, project management, and communication platforms — are documented, current, and not dependent on owner access credentials. A competent buyer could assume operational control of all systems within 30 days of close. Systems are in place but documentation is incomplete or access is concentrated with the owner or a key employee. Some manual workarounds exist. Buyers will require a systems inventory and transition plan as part of the diligence process. Operations are managed through informal tools, personal accounts, or systems that are not documented or transferable. This creates material transition risk and will require buyer-funded remediation post-close, reducing the effective price realized by the seller.
