The Problem

The Headline Number Is Not the Check.

Most business owners focus almost entirely on the purchase price multiple when thinking about their exit. The multiple matters — but it's only one input into how much you actually receive. The rest is in the terms.

In a lower middle market transaction, the enterprise value is where negotiations begin. What the seller receives at closing — and over time — is shaped by a series of adjustments, holdbacks, and contingencies that most owners encounter for the first time while the clock is running on an active process.

The guide is designed to give you a working vocabulary for those terms before you need it.

Common Questions

What Owners Ask Before Downloading

Is this guide relevant if I'm not planning to sell for several years?

Yes — and it's arguably more useful the further out you are. Understanding deal structure before you're in a process allows you to ask better questions when you engage advisors, evaluate term sheets without time pressure, and structure your business in ways that reduce the structural leverage buyers typically use. Owners who learn these terms mid-process are negotiating against the clock.

Does this guide cover all the terms in a purchase agreement?

No. A purchase agreement in a lower middle market transaction is a complex legal document covering hundreds of specific provisions. This guide focuses on the five areas of deal structure that most directly affect seller economics — what you receive, when you receive it, and under what conditions. It is not a substitute for qualified transaction counsel.

How common are earnouts in lower middle market transactions?

Earnouts appear in a significant proportion of lower middle market transactions — particularly those involving private equity buyers and businesses with near-term growth drivers not fully reflected in trailing financials. Their frequency has increased in recent years as financing conditions have tightened. Not all deals include earnouts, but sellers who don't understand the mechanics are at a disadvantage when they appear.

Can I negotiate these terms, or are they standard?

All of these terms are negotiable — to varying degrees, depending on deal size, buyer type, and the seller's leverage in the process. The guide includes market-standard benchmarks for each term so you understand what is typical and where negotiation has historically moved the needle for sellers. Having a qualified M&A advisor in the process significantly improves the seller's position on structural terms.
